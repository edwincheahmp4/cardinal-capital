This Week

The week of June 13–20 was one for the history books. SpaceX went public in the largest IPO ever recorded, raising $75 billion and vaulting past a $2 trillion market cap on its first day of trading. Meanwhile, a hawkish turn from new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh rattled bond and tech markets. And President Trump announced the end of the U.S.-Iran conflict, sending oil lower and stocks higher. When the dust settled, the S&P 500 logged its 11th winning week in the last 12.

Top Stories

IPO · Technology

SpaceX Lands on Wall Street — and Immediately Hits the Moon

Elon Musk's rocket company made its long-awaited stock market debut on June 12, pricing at $135 per share and raising a record $75 billion — more than triple the previous all-time IPO record set by Alibaba in 2014. Trading under the ticker SPCX on the Nasdaq, shares opened at $150 and closed the first day up 19%, pushing the company's market cap above $2 trillion and briefly making Musk the world's first trillionaire.

The rally extended into the following week, with SPCX adding another 20% on its first full Monday of trading. At its peak, SpaceX ranked as the seventh-largest U.S. company by market cap — ahead of Tesla. The offering drew enormous retail interest, though most individual investors received few shares in the allocation, leaving many facing a familiar dilemma: chase the momentum or wait for a pullback.

An IPO is when a private company sells shares to the public for the first time. The "pop" on day one often reflects years of pent-up demand from investors who couldn't own the stock before. Watch how SPCX trades over the next several weeks — that's when the real signal emerges.

Macro · Federal Reserve

The Fed's New Sheriff Signals Rates Could Go Higher — Not Lower

Bond traders received Kevin Warsh's message loud and clear. In his first meeting as Federal Reserve Chair, Warsh presided over a unanimous decision to hold the federal funds rate at 3.50%–3.75% — then used the press conference to signal that the next move might be a hike, not a cut.

The Fed's updated "dot plot" showed that nine of 18 officials now expect at least one rate increase in 2026, with the median year-end estimate rising to 3.8% from 3.4% in March. Warsh repeatedly invoked "price stability" — Wall Street shorthand for a Fed that won't blink on inflation. Two-year Treasury yields, which closely track rate expectations, surged 13 basis points on the day to 4.18%, the largest single-day move in more than a year.

Tech stocks led the selling on Wednesday — higher rates make future earnings worth less today, and growth stocks feel it first. But markets found their footing by Thursday. The Nasdaq ended the week up 2.4%, carried largely by SpaceX's gravitational pull. The broader lesson: a hawkish Fed doesn't always tank markets in the same week — but it reshapes the calculus for what you want to own.

Geopolitics · Energy

U.S. and Iran Reach Peace Deal — Blockade Lifted, Oil Slides

President Trump announced over the weekend that the U.S. and Iran had reached an agreement to end their months-long military conflict — a war that had sent oil above $110 a barrel and rattled global shipping routes. U.S. forces immediately began lifting the maritime blockade on Iranian ports, and traders wasted no time pricing in the return of Iranian supply to global markets.

The news triggered a broad risk-on rally to open Monday. Travel and leisure names jumped — Carnival, Royal Caribbean, and Norwegian Cruise Line each rose roughly 2%. The Dow Jones briefly cracked 52,000 for the first time ever. Brent crude settled near $92.56/barrel, well off its 2026 peak, as the risk premium built up over months of conflict began to unwind.

The oil move is a double-edged sword. Lower energy costs are good for consumers, airlines, and manufacturers — but energy sector stocks gave back gains as the pricing power that had buoyed them for months receded. Markets are also watching carefully: a brief diplomatic breakthrough in March had briefly eased tensions before they re-escalated. The Strait of Hormuz, one of the world's most critical oil chokepoints, remains as strategically sensitive as ever.

Portfolio Pulse

Themes and trades firms are watching

Rotate to "Old Economy"

With rates expected to stay elevated — and potentially move higher under Warsh — many strategists are tilting toward sectors with tangible pricing power: energy, industrials, materials, and utilities. These businesses can pass on higher costs to customers. High-multiple growth stocks, by contrast, are most sensitive to rate increases that erode the present value of their future earnings.

The AI & Tech IPO Supercycle

SpaceX was the opening act. The pipeline of pending AI and tech IPOs is estimated at $3.6 trillion, with names like OpenAI and Anthropic among those eyeing the public markets. Bankers are watching SPCX's post-debut trajectory closely — a sustained rally would signal institutional appetite for the next wave of listings through late 2026 and into 2027. Most IPO shares still go to institutional buyers first; retail access remains a practical challenge.

Watch Duration Risk Heading Into H2

Warsh's debut press conference was a subtle but meaningful shift. He gave no forward guidance suggesting cuts are coming and repeatedly leaned on "price stability" language. Several Wall Street desks are now penciling in at least one rate hike in Q3 or Q4 2026. That puts a spotlight on duration risk — owning long-dated bonds or rate-sensitive growth stocks into a period of potential tightening is a position worth sizing carefully.
